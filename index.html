Cross-Asset Interpretation · Week of Feb 23–27, 2026

The week's defining catalyst: a scorching January PPI report released Friday (headline +0.5% MoM vs. +0.3% est.; core +0.8% MoM vs. +0.3% est.) confirmed that inflation remains far from tamed, triggering an immediate selloff. The S&P 500 fell −0.44% on the week, the Nasdaq −0.95%, and the VIX surged above 20 intraday — capping a brutal February where the Nasdaq posted its worst monthly loss since March 2025 (−3%+) and the IGV software ETF shed nearly 10% in February alone.

Compounding the inflation shock, the "AI scare trade" accelerated throughout the week. Monday opened with Anthropic unveiling Claude Code — an AI tool automating work typically done by consulting firms — sending IBM −13%, Accenture, and CrowdStrike sharply lower. Nvidia beat spectacularly Wednesday ($68.1B revenue, +73% YoY), but the stock fell over 5% Thursday in a classic "sell the news" as investors demanded clarity on ROI from the $650B+ hyperscaler capex cycle. The week ended with a painful paradox: the best quarter in Nvidia history was not enough to sustain the AI trade.

Gold surged +2.94% to a fresh record $5,230/oz — its best weekly performance in months — driven by the combination of safe-haven demand (U.S.–Iran nuclear talks in Geneva stalled, technical talks moved to Vienna), PPI inflation feeding debasement concerns, and continued central bank buying. Meanwhile Bitcoin fell −4.49% to $64,952, now down −25.78% YTD. The 10-year Treasury yield dropped to 3.962% (−12.4 bps WoW), pricing in a "growth scare" override of the inflation signal — a stagflation setup that kept the Fed's hands tied. Credit spreads widened modestly; BBB OAS +4bps and HY OAS +3.47%.

🌡️ Inflation Alarm: PPI January 2026

Core Wholesale Prices Surge +0.8% — Biggest Miss in Months

The BLS released the January Producer Price Index on Friday Feb 27 at 8:30am ET. Headline PPI rose +0.5% MoM (est. +0.3%), accelerating from December's +0.4%. Core PPI (ex-food/energy) surged +0.8% — more than double the +0.3% consensus — and the highest monthly gain since July 2025. Year-over-year, core PPI hit 3.6% (up from 3.3% in December).

The driver was services inflation (+0.8% MoM), particularly a 14.4% jump in professional/commercial equipment wholesaling margins. Goods prices fell −0.3% (energy −2.7%), but core goods still rose +0.7%. The report complicates PCE forecasting for March 13: several PCE components use PPI as direct inputs (healthcare, financial services). Markets immediately repriced Fed cut expectations further out.

Context: CPI Jan +2.4% YoY Core CPI +2.5% YoY Core PCE Dec +2.7% YoY Core PPI Jan +3.6% YoY — all above the Fed's 2% target.

Sources: BLS PPI Jan 2026 (Feb 27) · BLS CPI Jan 2026 (Feb 13) · BEA PCE (Feb 20)

Central bank demand continues as a structural bid, while ETF inflows have accelerated. The gold/equity divergence this week (stocks ↓, gold ↑ sharply) signals that market participants are hedging rather than risk-on positioning.

📊 Labor Market & Consumer Data

Jobs Resilient, Confidence Ticked Up — But Cracks Forming

Continuing claims fell 31,000 to 1,833,000, near a 10-month low. The labor market remains in a "low hire / low fire" equilibrium, with federal employee claims still elevated (554 per week) but declining from shutdown-period highs.

Conference Board Consumer Confidence for February ticked up to 91.2 (from 89.0 revised in January), driven by slightly improved expectations (Expectations Index 72.0 from 67.2). However, the index remains well below the November 2024 peak of 112.8. Consumers are still cautious about discretionary spending; restaurants and travel plans softened. Housing data (new home sales Dec 745K SAAR) and pending sales data show early signs of spring demand but remain constrained by elevated mortgage rates (~6.8%).

U.S. Market Narrative

Week of Feb 23–27, 2026

Equities: February Closes Painfully

U.S. equities ended February on a sour note, with all three major indices finishing the week lower. The S&P 500 lost −0.44% WoW to 6,878.88, remaining barely positive YTD (+0.49%). The Nasdaq, more heavily weighted in AI and technology names, fell −0.95% WoW to 22,668.21 — now down −2.47% YTD — posting its worst monthly performance since March 2025.

The week was defined by conflicting signals. Monday's session opened with an AI disruption shock after Anthropic released Claude Code. IBM collapsed 13% as markets priced in the threat to consulting-driven revenue. Through midweek, sentiment stabilized around Nvidia's earnings. But Thursday, despite reporting $68.1 billion in Q4 revenue (+73% YoY) and guiding Q1 to $78 billion, Nvidia fell over 5% — the "Nvidia Paradox," where flawless results were met with skepticism about the broader AI ROI story. Dell surged 21.8% on AI server demand, a lone bright spot in the tech complex.

Friday's hot PPI delivered the week's knockout blow. Futures dropped 400+ points at the open; the Dow closed −1.05% (−521 pts). Block (Square) added to the carnage with an announcement of 4,000+ layoffs (~half its workforce), raising fresh AI disruption concerns in fintech. The S&P 500 Equal Weight Index, however, touched a new all-time high Thursday — underscoring that the February selloff was concentrated in mega-cap tech, not broad market weakness.

Sources: CNBC Markets Live · Schwab Market Update (Feb 27) · Yahoo Finance

Inflation: PPI Breaks the Narrative

The week's dominant macro story was inflation. The January PPI, released Friday Feb 27, came in dramatically above expectations: headline +0.5% MoM (est. +0.3%), core +0.8% MoM (est. +0.3%). Year-over-year, core PPI accelerated to 3.6% — its highest reading since approximately mid-2025. Services inflation was the primary driver (+0.8% MoM), particularly a 14.4% surge in professional/commercial equipment wholesaling margins. Goods prices fell (energy −2.7%, food −1.5%), but core goods still rose +0.7%.

The PPI miss is consequential beyond the headline: multiple PCE components — the Fed's preferred inflation gauge — are computed directly from PPI subindices (healthcare, portfolio management, financial services). With PCE due March 13, markets now expect the January PCE to print firm, further delaying the Fed's rate-cutting timeline. The fed funds target remains 3.50–3.75%.

For full context: CPI January (released Feb 13) showed headline +0.2% MoM / +2.4% YoY; core CPI +2.5% YoY. PCE December (released Feb 20): headline +0.4% MoM / core +2.7% YoY. All measures remain above the 2% target with no clear convergence in sight.

Sources: BLS PPI Jan 2026 · BLS CPI Jan 2026 · BEA PCE (Feb 20)

Fixed Income: Rates Fall on Growth Fears

In a counterintuitive move given the hot PPI print, the 10-year Treasury yield fell −12.4 bps WoW to 3.962% — breaking below the psychologically important 4.00% level. This "growth-scare override" reflects markets pricing in that slowing U.S. growth (Q4 GDP +1.4%, well below the +2.5% consensus) outweighs near-term inflation pressures for longer-duration rates. Weak consumer confidence, AI disruption fears, and a potentially deteriorating labor market are weighing on the growth outlook.

The 3-month SOFR rate ticked down to 3.73371%, reflecting expectations for eventual Fed easing, while remaining well above neutral. The short-end of the curve is anchored by the Fed's 3.50–3.75% target rate. Credit spreads widened modestly: BBB OAS +4bps to 1.03%, and HY OAS +10bps to 2.98% — both still historically tight but directionally concerning heading into what could be a more volatile March. The 10-year TIPS yield (real yield) also fell, directly supporting gold's run to new highs.

Sources: Yahoo Finance (^TNX) · FRED SOFR · FRED HY OAS · Federal Reserve FOMC

Commodities & Currencies: Gold Record, Oil Steady

Gold was the week's standout performer, surging +2.94% to a new record $5,230.50/oz (+20.92% YTD). The rally was driven by a convergence of drivers: PPI-driven inflation expectations, U.S.–Iran geopolitical risk premium (third round of Geneva talks ended without deal; technical talks in Vienna to follow), declining real yields, and continued central bank purchasing. J.P. Morgan forecasts gold to average $5,055/oz by Q4 2026.

WTI crude rose +0.95% to $67.02/bbl (+16.72% YTD), a muted weekly move despite high-stakes Iran talks — because the Geneva talks ended with an agreement to continue diplomacy (Vienna technical level talks), which pared the risk premium somewhat. Oil markets remain highly sensitive to any breakdown in negotiations. Bitcoin fell −4.49% to $64,952, now down −25.78% YTD — continuing its decoupling from risk assets and being hit by risk-off flows from the PPI shock. EUR/USD gained +0.29% as a weaker dollar backdrop (growth concerns) supported the euro; CHF/USD +0.27% reflected safe-haven flows into the franc.

Sources: Yahoo Finance Gold · CNBC Iran Talks · J.P. Morgan Gold Outlook

Week of Feb 23–27, 2026 · Transmission to U.S. Markets
🌍 Geopolitics — Middle East

U.S.–Iran Nuclear Talks in Geneva: No Deal, Diplomacy Continues

The third round of indirect U.S.–Iran nuclear negotiations took place in Geneva on February 26, mediated by Oman's Foreign Minister Badr al-Busaidi. Led by U.S. Special Envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner on the American side, the six-hour session was described by Iran's FM Abbas Araghchi as the "longest and most serious" round yet, with "good progress" on elements of an agreement.

However, no deal was announced. The U.S. demands included destruction of Iran's three main nuclear sites (Fordow, Natanz, Isfahan), surrender of enriched uranium to the U.S., and a permanent (no sunset clause) deal. Iran rejected uranium transfer and insisted on enrichment continuation. Both sides agreed to technical-level talks in Vienna the following week. The U.S. maintains its largest Middle East military buildup in decades.

Market Transmission: Oil prices rose on geopolitical risk but pared gains Friday after the "talks to continue" signal. Gold surged on safe-haven demand and Strait of Hormuz risk premium. The risk of military confrontation remains the highest since 2019, acting as a persistent oil/gold floor.

Sources: CNBC · Bloomberg · NPR / AP
🤖 Technology — AI Disruption

Anthropic's Claude Code & the "AI Scare Trade" Accelerates

On Monday Feb 23, Anthropic released Claude Code — an agentic AI tool designed to automate analysis and complex tasks typically performed by consulting teams. The announcement immediately triggered what analysts are calling the "AI scare trade": IBM fell 13% (as markets priced disruption to its IT consulting business), Accenture and Cognizant dropped, and software stocks broadly sold off. Microsoft fell 3%, CrowdStrike −10%.

The Claude Code release follows weeks of AI disruption anxiety across sectors — trucking/logistics, commercial real estate, financial services, and software have all seen elevated selling pressure in February. The underlying concern: that agentic AI reduces headcount and software seats simultaneously, compressing margins across a wide range of industries.

Market Transmission: IGV (iShares Software ETF) fell nearly 10% in February alone (−23% YTD). The AI disruption narrative is becoming a macro-level factor, not just a sector story — with implications for employment, corporate spending, and GDP composition.

Sources: Yahoo Finance · CNBC (Feb 23)
🏭 Earnings — Technology

Nvidia Q4 FY2026: Record $68.1B Revenue, Stock Falls Anyway

Nvidia reported Q4 FY2026 results Wednesday evening (Feb 25): revenue $68.1B (+73% YoY), far exceeding the $65.6B consensus. Q1 guidance of $78B also crushed the Street. Data center revenue was $58.4B. CEO Jensen Huang described AI as entering the "agentic era," with compute demand growing exponentially.

Despite the historic beat, NVDA fell over 5% Thursday in a classic "sell the news" event — now called the "Nvidia Paradox." Investors are scrutinizing whether $650B+ in annual hyperscaler AI capex (Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Microsoft) will generate sufficient revenue to justify the investment. The stock's inability to rally on record results signals a maturing of the AI trade and rising demand for monetization proof points beyond chatbots.

Market Transmission: Nvidia's response dragged Broadcom, Oracle, and CrowdStrike 2%+ lower Friday. The AI infrastructure complex — which drove most of the 2024–2025 bull market — is now in a period of consolidation and scrutiny.

Sources: Fortune · CNBC Nvidia Earnings
🌐 Trade Policy — Tariffs

Post-SCOTUS Tariff Landscape: New 15% Baseline, EU Pushes Back

Following the Feb 20 Supreme Court ruling striking down the IEEPA "Liberation Day" tariffs, President Trump announced a replacement 15% global baseline tariff (raised from the 10% announced immediately post-ruling), effective immediately. He warned countries attempting to "play games" would face "much higher tariffs." The EU rejected any increase, stating "a deal is a deal" and calling on Washington to clarify its legal basis.

The tariff uncertainty continues to weigh on global trade outlooks. The Yale Budget Lab estimates the replacement tariffs will add 0.4–0.6% to CPI over 12 months. Apparel and goods PPI components moved higher in the January data, providing the first direct data evidence of tariff pass-through into wholesale prices. UBS downgraded U.S. equities to Neutral on tariff and growth uncertainty, shifting to Europe and EM overweights.

Market Transmission: MSCI World ex-U.S. has gained +8% YTD vs. S&P 500 nearly flat — the dollar's weakness and cheaper non-U.S. valuations are drawing capital abroad. The tariff backdrop complicates Fed policy: goods disinflation is being offset by tariff-driven goods repricing.
